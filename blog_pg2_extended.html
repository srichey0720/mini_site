Zambian Space Race

May 2022

In 1964, at the height of the space race between the U.S. and the former Soviet Union, an unlikely competitor was plotting to enter the competition. Edwuard Mukuka Nkoloso, a Zambian science teacher, started the unofficial Zambia National Academy of Science, Space Research, and Philosophy, tapping students from local schools and youth groups to join the lunar training program. Nkoloso dubbed them “Afronauts”, and had the trainees perform exercises that included rolling down hills in an oil drum and launching themselves from tree swings to simulate weightlessness. Their space suits reportedly consisted of green satin jackets and capes made from ornate African fabrics. Western reporters caught wind of the story, but most reported on it incredulously, characterizing Nkoloso as a “crackpot” and the program as a laughable lost cause (Serpell, 2017). The Afronauts never made it to the moon. Nkoloso was unable to secure funding and Matha Mwamba, the 16 year old girl they intended to launch along with her six cats, got pregnant by another student and returned home. The program was disbanded, and the story forgotten.

Half a century later, Spanish photographer Cristina de Middel unearthed old footage of the Zambian space program. Looking through the past coverage, she bristled against the stereotypical representations of African people as uncivilized and the victims of tragic circumstance, particularly after discovering program documents and images that held clues to the contrary (Schuman, 2013).

She subsequently put out The Afronauts, a 2013 photobook which reimagines the story anew. De Middel’s portrayal offers a more suggestive and contextual retelling of this unconventional story, elevating the afronaut’s pursuit by likening it to the dreams of other nations that led to progress rather than reducing it to a fool’s errand. In doing so, she highlights the inherent subjectivity of the previous photographic coverage, critiquing the notion that photography is a medium of objective truth. By including meticulously recreated letters and documents she underscored the power of design in providing adequate contextual information to create more nuanced and representative narratives.

The long held notion that a photograph serves as an artifact of truth ignores all of the circumstances and decisions that precede and follow the taking of a picture, the contextual information essential for understanding. Consider the famous photograph by Eddie Adams of Nguyen Van Lem, a Viet Cong soldier, being shot in the head by Nguyen Ngoc Loan, a South Vietnamese general. The photo went on to be printed and broadcast by news organizations around the world as an artifact of the brutality of the Vietnamese. But as Adams himself notes, "photography by its nature is selective. It isolates a single moment, divorcing that moment from the moments before and after that possibly lead to adjusted meaning" (Eddie Adams' iconic photo, 2018). Adams notes that initially, he too interpreted the general actions as cold and calculating. But later, he learned that Lem had killed the family of one of Loan’s fellow soldiers. South Vietnam had just been ambushed by the Viet Cong’s Tet Offensive and "if you hesitate, if you don't do your duty, the men won't follow you," the general later explained to Adams (Eddie Adams' iconic photo, 2018). It would be impossible for a single image to capture the entirety of these circumstances. And upon learning them, the truth becomes much more complicated and open to interpretation.

This is why providing adequate context is so essential. De Middel balances the fantastical nature of her images as well as our own preconceived notions about the feasibility of an African space program through design. By presenting the story as a technical manual, rife with recreations of historical program documents detailing correspondence with the Zambian government, technical drawings of the propulsion system, and the components of their spacesuits, a new picture emerges. These elements reveal a program that doesn’t seem like something to be ridiculed or pitied, but a pursuit to be admired considering their lack of resources.

De Middel doesn’t mean to suggest that the Zambians were likely to make it to the moon. Rather she makes the case that the codified visual shorthand so often relied upon in photography and media to quickly get one’s point across is often misleading and inadequate in telling a whole story. To convey a story that holds a multitude of subjective truths is ultimately more representative of reality, and of humanity. De Middel argues that one should use all the tools in their arsenal to achieve telling these truths, including design.
